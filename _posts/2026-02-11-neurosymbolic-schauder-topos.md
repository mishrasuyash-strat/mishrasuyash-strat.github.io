This one is a synthesis paper — a white paper connecting two things that do not
usually sit on the same page.

The first is a genuine breakthrough in pure analysis: De Filippis and Mingione's
resolution of the sharp growth-rate conjecture in nonuniformly elliptic Schauder
theory, which pins down the exact threshold $q/p < 1 + \alpha/n$ for gradient
Hölder continuity, using a subtle "ghost equation" that sidesteps the
non-differentiability of the usual Euler–Lagrange systems. The mathematics here
is theirs; my interest is in what it means for how such results get built and
checked.

The second is the rise of large reasoning models. My argument is that the next
phase of mathematical discovery comes from putting these together: reasoning
models grounded in topos theory and formal verification, where a proof becomes a
categorical colimit in a slice topos and the regularity bounds are
machine-checkable. It is a position piece about what a rigorous, verifiable
neurosymbolic mathematics could actually look like.

Full paper on arXiv: [2602.10632](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10632).
